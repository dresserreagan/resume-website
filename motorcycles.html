Why I Ride

Motorcycling isn't just a hobby — it's a mindset. When you're on a bike, every sense is alive: the rumble of the engine, the wind, the lean into a corner. There's no radio, no notifications, no distractions. It's just you, the machine, and the road ahead.

I got my license as a way to explore and push outside my comfort zone — and it's become one of the most rewarding decisions I've made. Whether it's a quick canyon run or a long highway cruise, riding clears my head like nothing else.

It also teaches you patience and situational awareness. You have to anticipate what drivers around you are going to do, read the road surface, manage your speed, and stay calm. Those habits carry into everything — sales calls, team leadership, school.

Gear & Pre-Ride Prep

Every ride starts before the engine turns over. Here's how I gear up and do a safety check:

  1. Essential Riding Gear (ATGATT — All The Gear, All The Time)
    • Full-face helmet (DOT/ECE rated)
    • Armored riding jacket (CE Level 2 back and shoulder protectors)
    • Riding gloves with knuckle protection
    • Over-the-ankle boots with ankle support
    • Riding pants with knee and hip armor
  2. T-CLOCS Pre-Ride Inspection
    • Tires — check pressure and tread depth
    • Controls — clutch, throttle, levers, cables
    • Lights — headlight, brake light, turn signals
    • Oil — engine oil level and brake fluid
    • Chassis — frame, chain/belt tension, fasteners
    • Stands — side stand and center stand (if equipped)
  3. Route Planning
    • Check weather — rain makes roads slippery, especially the first hour
    • Know your fuel range and where the next station is
    • Share your route with someone if riding solo
